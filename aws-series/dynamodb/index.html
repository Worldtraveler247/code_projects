Amazon DynamoDB

Serverless NoSQL key-value & document database. Single-digit-millisecond reads and writes at any scale — from your first table to Amazon.com on Prime Day. No servers, no version upgrades, no capacity-planning meetings.

01 What is DynamoDB?

DynamoDB is a fully managed, serverless NoSQL database. You define a table with a partition key (required) and an optional sort key, and AWS handles the rest — sharding, replication, failover, scaling, patching, backups. Reads and writes return in milliseconds whether your table holds a thousand items or a hundred billion.

It's not a relational database. There are no joins, no foreign keys, no SQL (well — there's PartiQL, but you'll rarely use it for hot paths). Instead you design your table around the access patterns you'll need — sometimes called single-table design — and DynamoDB rewards you with predictable performance forever.

The objective: predictable performance at any scale

Traditional databases get slower as they grow. Indexes bloat, query planners pick worse plans, the buffer cache stops fitting in RAM. The 100-row query that ran in 2 ms at launch takes 200 ms with 100 million rows.

DynamoDB doesn't slow down with scale. Same 5 ms read at 10 GB or at 10 TB. That's the entire point. You give up SQL flexibility; you get a database that stops being the bottleneck.

Two billing modes you have to understand

  • On-demand — pay per request ($1.25 per million writes, $0.25 per million reads as of 2024). No capacity planning. Scales instantly. Best for new apps and unpredictable workloads.
  • Provisioned — pay for reserved RCU/WCU per second. Cheaper at sustained predictable load. Eligible for free tier (25 RCU + 25 WCU + 25 GB) and 1-year/3-year reserved capacity discounts.
In one sentence

DynamoDB is a NoSQL database that scales horizontally forever — and bills you per request — so the only thing you need to think about is how your app reads and writes data.

02 A vibrant example: Amazon.com on Prime Day

Amazon.com itself runs on DynamoDB. The shopping cart, the customer profile, the order history, the recommendation tags — all of it. They built DynamoDB because their relational databases couldn't keep up with holiday traffic, and then they sold it to the rest of us.

During Amazon Prime Day 2023, DynamoDB peaked at 126 million requests per second across Amazon's retail systems. No infrastructure scaling drama, no SREs paged at 3 a.m. for capacity. The graph of the load test went vertical and the database absorbed it. AWS Prime Day 2023 metrics →

Now imagine your startup gets featured on Hacker News at 9 a.m. and traffic spikes 200x. With Postgres on a single instance, you'd be debugging connection pool exhaustion and full-table scans by 9:15. With DynamoDB on-demand, the bill goes up and the latency doesn't move. That's why companies use it for anything that has to "just work" under load.

03 Who uses DynamoDB — and why

Pretty much anyone running a high-scale or unpredictably-bursty workload on AWS. Concrete examples (publicly documented):

🛒 Amazon.com

Shopping cart, order history, customer profile. DynamoDB was built for Amazon's own retail platform before it became a public service.

🎬 Disney+

Launched on DynamoDB in 2019 — onboarded 10M subscribers in the first 24 hours and 50M in the first three months without a database scaling pause.

🚗 Lyft

Ride state, driver location heartbeats, surge pricing data. Latency-sensitive — DynamoDB's predictable single-digit-ms reads are the design constraint.

🎮 Riot Games

League of Legends player profiles, match state, leaderboards. Geographic distribution via DynamoDB Global Tables across regions.

📸 Snap (Snapchat)

Maps, friend graph, story state. DynamoDB's TTL feature auto-expires ephemeral content — built for exactly this use case.

🌐 Serverless apps

Almost every Lambda + API Gateway app uses DynamoDB. No connection pool, no idle DB cost, scales to zero. The default serverless data store.

Common reasons to reach for DynamoDB

  • Predictable latency at scale. Same 5 ms at 1 GB or 1 PB.
  • Serverless apps. Lambda hates connection pools; DynamoDB doesn't have any.
  • Bursty / unpredictable traffic. On-demand mode makes a 100x spike a billing question, not an outage.
  • Auto-expiry data. Sessions, OTPs, ephemeral state — TTL deletes items for free.
  • Multi-region apps. Global Tables replicate across regions in seconds with single-digit-ms local reads.
  • Operational simplicity. No version upgrades. No "the DB ran out of disk." No nightly backup script. Genuinely zero ops.

When NOT to use DynamoDB

  • You need joins or complex aggregations across many entities → use RDS / Aurora / Postgres.
  • You need ad-hoc analytics queries → use Redshift, Athena, or ClickHouse.
  • Your access patterns are unknown or change weekly → DynamoDB rewards upfront design.
  • You need full-text search → use OpenSearch.
  • You don't trust the math on cost — at very high sustained volumes, self-hosted Cassandra or Postgres can be cheaper if you have the ops talent.

04 Competitors & positioning

Honest framing: there's no clean "managed NoSQL market share" number. AWS doesn't publish DynamoDB revenue separately, and MongoDB (the most-deployed NoSQL) is mostly self-hosted. What we can say:

Service Type Where it wins
AWS DynamoDB Managed NoSQL (key-value + document) Serverless, predictable latency at scale, free tier, deepest AWS integration. Loses on: data-modeling learning curve.
MongoDB Atlas Managed document DB Multi-cloud (AWS / Azure / GCP), richer query language, full-text search built in. Loses on: multi-region complexity, can hit scaling cliffs.
Google Firestore Managed document DB Real-time listeners, mobile/web SDK with offline sync. Best for client-direct apps.
Google Cloud Bigtable Wide-column / sparse Massive analytics ingestion (HBase-compatible). Different shape than DynamoDB; closer to Cassandra.
Azure Cosmos DB Multi-model, multi-API 5 consistency levels, multi-API (SQL / Mongo / Cassandra / Gremlin / Table). Loses on: price at scale.
Apache Cassandra / ScyllaDB Self-hosted wide-column No vendor lock-in, can run anywhere, ScyllaDB is faster than vanilla Cassandra. Loses on: you operate it.

Popularity scores: DB-Engines Ranking tracks "popularity" via mentions, jobs, and traffic — not revenue. DynamoDB is consistently #1 in their Cloud-only DBMS category. MongoDB tops the broader Document Stores ranking.

The honest "why DynamoDB"

If you're already on AWS and you need a key-value or document store, DynamoDB is almost always the lowest-friction choice — IAM auth, VPC endpoints, free tier, no database to keep running. The cost is upfront data-modeling work. If you're multi-cloud or your access patterns are still in flux, MongoDB Atlas is the safer bet.

05 Who actually runs it for you?

DynamoDB is unusual: there's almost nothing to "operate." The interesting work shifts upstream — to data modeling and capacity planning — and downstream — to monitoring and cost.

👤 Self-managed (solo / startup)

You design the table, write the access patterns, watch the CloudWatch metrics. There is no server to patch, no failover to test. Easiest learning curve of any AWS database.

🛠️ App / platform team

Platform team typically owns Terraform modules for tables (PITR enabled, KMS, alarms baked in). App teams declare a table via PR rather than ClickOps in the Console.

📊 Data modeling specialist

At scale, the bottleneck becomes table design. Companies hire or contract DynamoDB data-modeling experts (Alex DeBrie, Rick Houlihan and AWS Solutions Architects do this kind of work). Watch their re:Invent talks.

🛡️ Security / compliance

Encryption at rest with customer-managed KMS keys, fine-grained IAM (item / attribute level), CloudTrail data events on writes, PITR for forensic restore. Your security team owns these knobs.

💰 FinOps

DynamoDB cost is straightforward but has gotchas: GSIs double the write cost, on-demand is 5–7x more per request than provisioned at sustained load. FinOps reviews sustained tables for mode switches.

🤖 No "AWS Pro Services" angle

Unlike EC2, there's no real "let AWS run it" tier — DynamoDB is already that tier. AWS Solutions Architects help with data modeling and migrations, but day-to-day is fully self-service.

⚠️ Cost gotchas

Scans are expensive. A full-table Scan reads every item and bills you for every one. Scans on a 10 GB table cost real money; on a 10 TB table they're a budget event. GSIs (Global Secondary Indexes) double your write cost per index — that's the price of the alternate query path. Plan accordingly.

06 Security mindset

DynamoDB has fewer foot-guns than EC2 or S3, but it has its own subtle ones. The misconfigurations show up at the IAM layer and the access pattern layer — not at the database itself.

Five things to internalize

  • Use fine-grained IAM, not dynamodb:*. DynamoDB supports condition keys that scope an IAM policy down to specific items based on the partition key — e.g. a Lambda can only read items where userId = ${aws:userid}. This is a superpower most teams don't use.
  • Encryption at rest is on by default — but pick your key. AWS-owned key is free but invisible in your account. AWS-managed key appears in KMS. Customer-managed key (CMK) gives you key rotation, key deletion, and CloudTrail visibility on every cryptographic operation. Use CMK for anything regulated.
  • Enable Point-in-Time Recovery (PITR). 35-day continuous backup, restore to any second. The "I just DELETE'd the prod table" recovery story. ~20% extra storage cost and worth it.
  • Use VPC Endpoints (Gateway type) for private access. Free, eliminates NAT cost for DynamoDB traffic from private subnets, and lets you write a resource policy that only allows access from your VPC.
  • CloudTrail data events log every PutItem / GetItem. Off by default (volume reasons). Turn them on for sensitive tables — forensic gold during incident response.
For your career

DynamoDB is on the AWS Solutions Architect and Developer exams. Security Specialty asks about CMK encryption, IAM condition keys (item-level access), and VPC endpoints for DynamoDB. The real-world skill underneath those questions is "design a secure access pattern for a multi-tenant table." Practice that mental model.
